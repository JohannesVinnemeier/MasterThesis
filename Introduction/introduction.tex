\section{Introduction}

The question of whether a closed manifold admits a metric of positive scalar curvature has been a central topic in differential geometry over the past decades and remains an area of active research. One part of obtaining an answer to this question consists in finding suitable obstructions to the existence of positive scalar curvature metrics. For example, in dimension 2, the Euler characteristic of a closed manifold provides such an obstruction by the Gauss--Bonnet Theorem, and by the classification of surfaces this is, in fact, the only obstruction. In higher dimensions, however, this problem becomes much more subtle. One way to still approach it is by means of index theory of the spin Dirac operator. In 1980 Gromov and Lawson \cite{gromov1980spin} used this technique to identify a large class of manifolds which do not admit a metric of positive scalar curvature, namely so-called enlargeable manifolds. We will give a precise definition of this notion below, from which we will easily derive the following two facts:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
    \item $S^1$ is enlargeable,
    \item Products of enlargeable manifolds are enlargeable.
\end{enumerate}

From this it follows at once that the $n$-Torus $T^n$ is enlargeable and thus does not admit a metric of positive scalar curvature in all dimensions $n \geq 1$, a result that had long remained an open question. This example already highlights the importance of the concept of enlargeability, which, since its emergence, has been a subject of extensive research. More generally, the two facts above show that if $M$ is enlargeable, $M \times S^1$ does not admit a metric of positive scalar curvature. There are two natural ways one may try to generalize this statement. Firstly, we can ask what happens if $M$ is replaced by an arbitrary non-enlargeable manifold that does not admit a metric of positive scalar curvature. Naively, one might expect the statement to still be true, nonetheless it turns out that there are counterexamples, see \cite[Remark 1.4]{cecchini2023nonnegative}, \cite[Counterexample 4.16]{stolz2014metrics}. Secondly, we can ask what happens if the $S^1$-factor appears as the fiber of some non-trivial circle bundle over $M$. However, once again, this seemingly plausible expectation does not hold. Counterexamples to this second question have recently been constructed in \cite{kumar2025circle} using techniques from symplectic geometry. In the following we will provide an alternative construction for such counterexamples, relying only on basic topological methods.
